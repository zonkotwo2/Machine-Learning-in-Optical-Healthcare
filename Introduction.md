# Introduction
In the field of neuroimaging in recent years, there have been many advancements in brain mapping to understand and view the different complexities within it. Advances in neuroimaging technologies have revolutionised the study of brain function, allowing researchers to map neural activity, quantify cerebral blood flow (CBF).

This study aims to show the significance of cerebral blood flow and brain state with the use of different preprocessing techniques and selective classifiers, focusing on how visual stimuli influence haemodynamic activity. Blood flow is recorded with a SWIR (short-wave infrared) laser paired with a high frames per second camera directed at the nape of the participant, the anatomical location of the occipital lobe. The acquired frames are analysed using multiple data processing techniques to compare their effectiveness in quantifying CBF and classifying brain states.

Optical flow approach captures the motion of brightness patterns across consecutive frames, providing temporal insights into CBF dynamics. Contrast correlation quantifies variations in blood flow intensity within individual frames by calculating the ratio of standard deviation to mean intensity, identifying regions with high or low blood flow. Cross-correlation, on the other hand, measures the similarity between successive frames to track the degree of change and the magnitude of haemodynamic shifts. These methods are compared to assess their computational efficiency, robustness, and accuracy in analysing cerebral blood flow. Finally, machine learning algorithms are applied to the processed data from each method to classify brain states, providing an evaluation of how the choice of data processing technique impacts the model’s performance and accuracy.

This study benefits the future of neuroimaging by providing a verifiable standard of work that shows neural activity and functional brain mapping can be explored remotely with signal processing software rather than more expensive equipment on location with the patient.
